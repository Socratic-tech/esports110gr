Cultural Analysis

How does this game reflect, reinforce, or challenge culture?

Cultural analysis examines a game as an artifact of the society that made it. Every design choice — from who the protagonist is to what behaviors are rewarded — reflects assumptions about the world.

What to look for: Values & Assumptions

What does the game treat as normal? What worldview does it reward? Games often embed ideological assumptions so deeply that players never notice them. Your job is to notice.

What to look for: Historical Context

When was this game made? What was happening in gaming culture, broader culture, or the world? Great analysis connects a text to its moment of production.

What to look for: Reception & Meaning-Making

Different players experience the same game differently. Who is the assumed audience? Who was left out? How did communities actually interpret and use this game?

Key vocabulary: ideology, hegemony, cultural artifact, reception, discourse, normalization, othering, the male gaze, power fantasy.

Professional Example — Cultural Lens

"Red Dead Redemption 2 is obsessed with American mythology — the frontier, manifest destiny, the honorable outlaw — and it both celebrates and deconstructs that mythology simultaneously. The game knows the West it depicts was built on displacement and violence, and it forces you to participate in that violence even as it mourns it. This is not a western that glorifies the genre uncritically; it's a eulogy for it."

— Adapted from critical analysis tradition

Why this works: The writer identifies a specific cultural mythology the game engages with (the American West), names the ideology embedded in that mythology (manifest destiny, the honorable outlaw), and then shows how the game's design creates a complicated relationship to it rather than simple celebration. The analysis moves beyond "this game has western themes" to "here's what those themes mean and how the game handles them."

Notice: The writer makes a claim about intent and effect. They don't just describe — they interpret. That's the move you're practicing.

Use these questions as your thinking prompts. You don't need to answer all of them — pick the 2–3 that open up the richest discussion for your specific game.

  • What cultural mythology or narrative does this game participate in? (Hero's journey? Frontier conquest? Rags-to-riches? Survival of the fittest?)
  • What values does succeeding at this game require you to practice or endorse?
  • What does the game treat as "natural" or "normal" that is actually a constructed cultural choice?
  • Where was this game made, and how does that national or corporate context shape its worldview?
  • How did different communities of players interpret this game differently from the developers' intent?
  • Does the game engage with historical events or systems? How accurately and responsibly?
  • What anxieties or desires of its cultural moment does this game express?
Strong claim structure: "[Game] participates in [cultural mythology/discourse] by [specific mechanic or design choice], which [effect/implication]." Example: "Fortnite participates in consumer culture by making cosmetic purchases the primary vehicle for identity expression, which normalizes the idea that self-presentation requires spending."

Design Philosophy

What does this game believe about what games should be and do?

Every game embeds a theory of what games are for. Some believe games should challenge and punish failure. Others believe games should remove friction and maximize accessibility. Design philosophy analysis makes that theory explicit — and evaluates it.

MDA Framework: Mechanics → Dynamics → Aesthetics

Mechanics are the rules. Dynamics are what emerges when players interact with those rules. Aesthetics are the emotional experiences that emerge from those dynamics. Academic game analysis often traces this chain to understand why a game feels the way it feels.

Intentionality & the Implied Player

Designers make choices for a specific imagined player. Who is the "implied player" this game was designed for? Are you that player? What happens when players outside that assumption encounter the game?

Design Lineage

No game exists in a vacuum. What games did this inherit from? What conventions did it break? How does understanding that lineage change how we evaluate the design choices?

Key vocabulary: MDA framework, flow state, implied player, affordance, friction, game feel, ludonarrative dissonance, emergent gameplay, procedural rhetoric.

Professional Example — Design Philosophy Lens

"Celeste makes a specific philosophical claim: failure is not punishment, it is information. Every death resets you instantly, with no load screens, no resource costs. The mechanic communicates a worldview — that trying is never penalized, only the absence of trying. This is not an accident of design. It is a coherent belief about what games can model about persistence."

— Adapted from critical analysis tradition

Why this works: The writer moves from a specific mechanic (instant respawn, no cost) to the philosophy that mechanic embeds (failure = information, not punishment) to the broader claim about what the game is arguing. This is design philosophy analysis at its best — it treats mechanical choices as meaningful statements.

Ian Bogost's concept of "procedural rhetoric": games make arguments through the systems they implement, not just through narrative or dialogue. A game about resource management argues something about scarcity. A game about violence with no consequences argues something about the morality of violence.

Choose questions that unlock the most interesting discussion for your specific game:

  • What theory of fun or engagement does this game's design embody? Is it challenge-based, narrative-based, social, exploratory, expressive?
  • What does the game's approach to failure say about its beliefs about learning or persistence?
  • Who is the "implied player"? Is that assumption limiting or enabling?
  • What argument does the game make through its systems — not its story, but its rules?
  • How does this game's design relate to or break from its genre's conventions?
  • Where does the game create "ludonarrative dissonance" — where narrative and mechanics contradict each other?
  • What does the pacing, difficulty curve, or moment-to-moment flow reveal about the design philosophy?
Strong claim structure: "[Game]'s [specific mechanic/system] embodies a belief that [design philosophy claim], which [result/implication for player experience]." Example: "Dark Souls' punishment economy — souls lost on death, recoverable but risky — embeds a belief that meaningful reward requires meaningful risk, which transforms every encounter into a negotiation between caution and greed."

Representation

Who is present, absent, centered, or marginalized in this game?

Representation analysis examines who gets to be a protagonist, who exists as a supporting character or enemy, and who is absent entirely. It also examines how groups are portrayed — with dignity and complexity, or as stereotypes.

The Spectrum of Representation

Representation exists on a spectrum from erasure (absent entirely) through stereotyping (present but reductive) to complexity (present with full humanity). Critical analysis identifies where on this spectrum a game sits — and asks why.

Character Agency vs. Function

Characters can exist to serve the protagonist (as rewards, obstacles, or tools) rather than having their own goals and interiority. Who has narrative agency in this game? Whose perspective does the camera take?

The Default and the "Other"

Games often have an assumed default player identity (frequently white, male, able-bodied, heterosexual). Everyone else is positioned as a variation, a choice, or an afterthought. Representation analysis names that default and examines what it costs.

Key vocabulary: the male gaze, othering, the Bechdel test (adapted for games), intersectionality, diversity vs. inclusion, tokenism, stereotyping, narrative agency, the default protagonist.

Professional Example — Representation Lens

"Hades is remarkable not for having diverse characters, but for what it does with them. Every character — regardless of gender, sexuality, or ethnicity — has a full arc, a distinct voice, and meaningful agency over their own story. The game treats queerness not as a plot point or a source of drama but as unremarkable — a baseline assumption rather than a revelation. This normalization is a design choice with real stakes."

— Adapted from critical analysis tradition

Why this works: The writer doesn't just say "Hades has diverse characters" (a surface observation). They analyze the quality of that representation — full arcs, distinct voices, narrative agency — and identify a specific design choice (normalizing queerness rather than centering it as drama) and its implications. Representation analysis at this level is about how, not just whether.

  • Who is the protagonist? What does that choice center and what does it exclude?
  • How are women, people of color, LGBTQ+ people, or disabled people represented — as complex characters or as functions/stereotypes?
  • Who has narrative agency in this game? Whose desires drive the story?
  • What groups are absent entirely — and what does that absence communicate?
  • How does the game frame violence or conflict along identity lines?
  • Can players customize their identity? What does the game allow or restrict, and what does that communicate?
  • Has the game's representation improved or regressed over sequels or updates? What does that trajectory reveal?
Important: Representation analysis is descriptive and interpretive, not prescriptive. The goal is to understand and explain, not to issue a verdict. Strong analysis acknowledges complexity — a game can represent one group well and another poorly simultaneously.

Industry & Economy

How do commercial forces shape this game's design?

Games are cultural objects, but they are also products made by companies operating under economic pressures. Understanding those pressures illuminates design choices that might otherwise seem arbitrary.

Business Models & Player Psychology

Free-to-play, subscription, live service, premium, DLC — different business models shape what gets designed. A free-to-play game with microtransactions has mechanical incentives to create desire and friction. Understanding the business model is understanding part of the design brief.

Labor & Production

Who made this game, under what conditions, and for what compensation? Crunch culture, studio closures, and the power asymmetries between publishers and developers are part of the critical picture of any commercial game.

Platform Economics

Games exist within ecosystems — console exclusives, storefronts, hardware requirements. These platform relationships shape what games can be made and who can access them. Platform analysis is economic analysis.

Key vocabulary: live service, microtransactions, crunch, AAA vs. indie, publisher/developer relationships, platform exclusivity, FOMO design, dark patterns, planned obsolescence, IP.

Professional Example — Industry Lens

"Destiny 2's seasonal model is not a content delivery system — it is an engineered anxiety machine. Content expires. FOMO is designed, not incidental. The game's 'live service' framing obscures that what's being sold is a subscription to perpetual incompleteness. Understanding this isn't cynicism; it's economic literacy about how the business model shapes every design decision."

— Adapted from critical analysis tradition

Why this works: The writer identifies a specific business model (live service seasonal content), names the psychological mechanism it deploys (FOMO, engineered anxiety), and reframes what the product actually is (subscription to incompleteness). This is industry analysis that moves from surface feature to underlying economic logic to player impact.

  • What is the business model, and how does it shape the design? (Microtransactions, DLC, subscription, premium, free-to-play)
  • Who published this game, and what commercial pressures did the publisher exert on the developer?
  • Is this a AAA, mid-tier, or indie game — and how does that production context shape what's possible?
  • Does the game use dark patterns (FOMO timers, loot boxes, artificial friction to drive spending)?
  • What do we know about the labor conditions that produced this game?
  • Is this game a platform exclusive? Who does that exclusivity benefit and who does it exclude?
  • How does the game's design reflect or resist the commercial pressures of its production context?
Strong claim structure: "[Game]'s [business model/commercial structure] creates [specific design incentive], which [effect on player experience or game culture]." Example: "FIFA's Ultimate Team loot box economy creates a design incentive to make skill gaps feel surmountable only through spending, which trains players to interpret losing as a purchasing problem rather than a skill problem."
