Phase 0: Game Proposal

Justifying Your Game Choice Before Analysis

Before you begin your detailed review, you'll write a brief proposal justifying which game you plan to analyze and why it's worth critical examination.

This isn't just a formality. Professionals in game journalism, academic research, and criticism always start by framing why a particular game matters—to the industry, to culture, to your argument.

Why This Matters

A game proposal does several things:

  • Demonstrates intentionality. You're not reviewing a game at random. You've chosen it because it reveals something meaningful about game design, industry trends, cultural impact, or artistic expression.
  • Establishes credibility. You show that you understand the game's context—its place in the industry, its audience, its design philosophy, or its cultural significance.
  • Frames your analysis. Your proposal tells readers (and yourself) what lens you'll use. Are you analyzing mechanical innovation? Cultural representation? Business impact? Artistic ambition?
  • Sets realistic scope. By proposing a game and an angle, you avoid trying to say everything about a game. Good analysis is focused.

What Makes a Game "Worthy of Analysis"?

You don't need a AAA blockbuster or a critically acclaimed indie darling. A worthy game for review is one where:

  • You can engage meaningfully with its design. You've played it (or can access it), understand its mechanics, and can describe how it actually plays.
  • It offers something to examine. The game does something interesting—mechanically, narratively, artistically, or culturally—that's worth discussing.
  • You can gather evidence. You can reference specific moments, systems, design choices, or industry context that support your claims.
  • It connects to your analytical perspective. You have something to say about it beyond "it's fun" or "it's bad."

Examples: A challenging indie platformer that reimagines movement. A narrative game that takes risks. A competitive esports title with interesting meta shifts. A mobile game that bridges accessibility and depth. A game that sparked controversy. A remake that recontextualizes the original. A title that influenced your understanding of game design.

Required Components

1. Game Title & Basic Information

Name, developer, release year, platform(s), and genre.

2. Justification & Relevance (1–2 paragraphs)

Why did you choose this game? What makes it worthy of professional analysis? What aspect of game design, culture, or industry does it illuminate?

3. Historical or Cultural Context (1 paragraph minimum)

Position your game within a broader context:

  • Industry context: What was happening in the industry when it launched? What trends does it follow or challenge?
  • Cultural context: How does it reflect or influence broader culture? Does it engage with representation, politics, or social commentary?
  • Design context: What design lineage does it belong to? What conventions does it follow or subvert?
  • Audience context: Who plays this game? Why? What does that tell us?

4. Your Analytical Approach (1 paragraph)

Preview how you'll analyze this game. What will you focus on?

  • Mechanical depth and challenge design?
  • Narrative structure and storytelling?
  • Art direction and visual language?
  • Sound design and audio worldbuilding?
  • Cultural or industry significance?
  • Accessibility and design philosophy?
  • A combination of these?

5. References (minimum 2–3)

Include sources that informed your proposal. These might be:

  • Professional reviews or coverage
  • Developer interviews or postmortems
  • Academic articles about the game or its genre
  • Industry analysis or trend reports
  • Cultural criticism or commentary

Use standard citation format (APA, MLA, or Chicago—check your syllabus).

Submission Expectations

Length

400–600 words (roughly 2–3 pages, single-spaced).

Format

Submit as a PDF, Word document, or Google Doc link through Canvas.

Style

Professional tone. Write as if you're pitching your game choice to a publication editor, not explaining it to a teacher. (Your instructor is reading as if they were that editor.)

Instructor Feedback

Your instructor will respond with one of the following:

  • Approved: Your game choice and approach are solid. Move forward with confidence.
  • Approved with feedback: Your proposal is approved, and here's guidance on refining your analytical angle.
  • Revision requested: We'd like to see a more developed proposal. Here's what to strengthen.

Important: You can begin the Training Quest while waiting for feedback. Learning how professional reviews work doesn't depend on your proposal being finalized.

Tips for Success

  • Be specific, not vague. "I'm reviewing a popular game" → "I'm analyzing *Elden Ring*'s difficulty design philosophy and how it shapes player agency."
  • Show your research. Cite real sources. Show that you know the game's context.
  • Demonstrate playing. Reference specific moments or systems. Show that you understand how the game actually works.
  • Avoid defensiveness. Don't propose a game because you think you're "supposed to" like it or because it's popular. Propose a game you genuinely want to examine.
  • Connect to the training. Use what you learn in the Training Quest to inform your proposal. Notice how professional reviewers frame their focus—apply that same thinking.

What's Next?
