Phase 0: Game Proposal

Justify Your Game Choice Before Analysis

Before you begin your detailed review, you'll write a brief proposal justifying which game you plan to analyze and why it's worth critical examination.

This isn't just a formality. Professionals in game journalism, academic research, and criticism always start by framing why a particular game matters—to the industry, to culture, to your argument.

Why This Matters

You're not reviewing a game at random. You've chosen it because it reveals something meaningful about game design, industry trends, cultural impact, or artistic expression.

Example: "I'm not reviewing *Elden Ring* because it's popular. I'm analyzing its difficulty design philosophy and how it creates meaningful player agency through mechanical challenge."

You show that you understand the game's context—its place in the industry, its audience, its design philosophy, or its cultural significance.

Example: "This game arrived during a moment when accessibility in challenging games was being debated. That context shapes its significance."

Your proposal tells readers (and yourself) what lens you'll use. Are you analyzing mechanical innovation? Cultural representation? Business impact? Artistic ambition?

Example: "My review focuses on *how the game teaches*, not whether it's fun."

By proposing a game and an angle, you avoid trying to say everything about a game. Good analysis is focused.

Example: You can't analyze every aspect of *The Legend of Zelda: Breath of the Wild* in one review. But you can powerfully analyze its environmental puzzle design.

What Makes a Game Worthy of Analysis?

You don't need a AAA blockbuster or a critically acclaimed indie darling. A worthy game for review is one where:

You've played it (or can access it), understand its mechanics, and can describe how it actually plays.

Strong choice: A game you've spent at least a few hours with and genuinely understand.

Weak choice: A game you've only watched on YouTube and don't fully grasp.

The game does something interesting—mechanically, narratively, artistically, or culturally—that's worth discussing.

Strong choice: A game with unique design, interesting story choices, or cultural significance.

Weak choice: A generic game with nothing distinctive to analyze.

You can reference specific moments, systems, design choices, or industry context that support your claims.

Strong choice: "In the boss fight with [specific boss], the game teaches you [specific mechanic]."

Weak choice: "The game is really good and innovative."

You have something to say about it beyond "it's fun" or "it's bad."

Strong choice: "This game demonstrates how difficulty can be a narrative device."

Weak choice: "I liked this game."

Example Proposals

Here are real proposal excerpts from professional reviewers and academics. Click to explore different approaches:

Strong Proposal

Game: Hollow Knight (2017), Indie Action-Adventure Metroidvania

Justification: Hollow Knight represents a critical moment in indie game design where mechanical mastery became a viable path to critical and commercial success. Unlike contemporary indie games that relied on narrative charm or visual innovation, Hollow Knight achieved both critical acclaim and commercial success primarily through superior mechanical design.

Analytical Approach: My review will focus on how Hollow Knight's deliberately restrictive combat system (one attack, one dodge, one parry) creates a foundation for precisely calibrated difficulty curves. I'll examine specific boss encounters as case studies in how limitation breeds mastery, and argue that the game's success demonstrates that mechanical depth can be the primary artistic and commercial vehicle for indie games.

Strong Proposal

Game: Control (2019), Remedy Entertainment

Justification: Control arrived in 2019 at a moment when AAA game narratives were increasingly questioned for thin storytelling despite massive budgets. Remedy instead invested heavily in environmental storytelling, document-based narrative, and cosmic horror worldbuilding—approaches traditionally reserved for indie games.

Context: Control's development coincided with broader industry conversations about what AAA games "should" do. By prioritizing narrative worldbuilding over cinematic setpieces, Remedy challenged AAA conventions.

Analytical Approach: I'll analyze how Control uses space, documents, and visual language to tell its story, arguing that narrative can emerge from environment rather than cutscenes. This approach has since influenced other AAA titles, making Control a pivotal example.

Strong Proposal

Game: What Remains of Edith Finch (2017), Giant Sparrow

Justification: This game represents an evolution in how video games handle grief and memory. Rather than traditional narrative mechanics (dialogue, cutscenes), the game communicates emotion through interaction, camera movement, and environmental storytelling.

Context: In 2017, narrative games were still debated within the industry. What Remains of Edith Finch demonstrated that deeply emotional stories could be told through pure play and exploration.

Analytical Approach: My review will examine how game mechanics themselves become narrative devices. I'll look at specific sequences and show how the mechanics chosen for each family story communicate thematic meaning—how you interact becomes as important as what you see.

Submission Expectations

Length

400–600 words (roughly 2–3 pages, single-spaced).

Format

Submit as a PDF, Word document, or Google Doc link through Canvas.

Style

Professional tone. Write as if you're pitching your game choice to a publication editor, not explaining it to a teacher. (Your instructor is reading as if they were that editor.)

Instructor Feedback

Your instructor will respond with one of the following:

  • Approved: Your game choice and approach are solid. Move forward with confidence.
  • Approved with feedback: Your proposal is approved, and here's guidance on refining your analytical angle.
  • Revision requested: We'd like to see a more developed proposal. Here's what to strengthen.

Important: You can begin the Training Quest while waiting for feedback. Learning how professional reviews work doesn't depend on your proposal being finalized.

Tips for Success

  • Be specific, not vague. "I'm reviewing a popular game" → "I'm analyzing *Elden Ring*'s difficulty design philosophy and how it shapes player agency."
  • Show your research. Cite real sources. Show that you know the game's context.
  • Demonstrate playing. Reference specific moments or systems. Show that you understand how the game actually works.
  • Avoid defensiveness. Don't propose a game because you think you're "supposed to" like it or because it's popular. Propose a game you genuinely want to examine.
  • Connect to the training. Use what you learn in the Training Quest to inform your proposal. Notice how professional reviewers frame their focus—apply that same thinking.
