# Training Quest Expansion – Specific Examples & Non-Examples

## What Was Expanded

The Training Quest (quest.html) now includes comprehensive examples and non-examples for each of the five review sections. Students can now see:

1. ✓ **Strong Examples** – What good analysis looks like
2. ✗ **What NOT to Do** – Common mistakes and weak analysis
3. **Explanation** – Why the examples work
4. **Practice** – Exercises to develop their skills

---

## New Tab Structure

Changed from 3 tabs to 4 tabs per section:

| Old | New |
|-----|-----|
| Model | ✓ Strong Examples |
| Explain | ✗ What NOT to Do |
| Practice | Explanation |
| — | Practice |

---

## What's in Each Section

### Introduction: How to Frame Your Analysis

**Strong Examples:**
- **Baldur's Gate 3** – Shows how to position a game in context while making an evaluative claim
- **Outer Wilds** – Demonstrates hooking the reader and contextualizing against current design trends

**What NOT to Do:**
- Generic praise ("This game is really good...")
- Pure description without argument (Wikipedia-style)
- Jargon masquerading as analysis
- Vague comparisons ("kind of like Dark Souls...")

---

### Gameplay: Analyzing Mechanics & Challenge

**Strong Examples:**
- **Hollow Knight** – Describes mechanics precisely, contextualizes within industry, explains design logic
- **Portal 2** – Shows how to analyze learning curves and mechanical progression

**What NOT to Do:**
- Vague description ("The gameplay is really good")
- Feature lists instead of analysis
- Personal preference instead of analysis ("I liked the combat")
- Confusing difficulty with bad design

---

### Graphics & Art Direction: Visual Language & Purpose

**Strong Examples:**
- **Return of the Obra Dinn** – Analyzes how 1-bit aesthetic serves the game's mystery
- **Control** – Shows how Brutalist architecture reinforces theme and meaning

**What NOT to Do:**
- Generic praise ("The graphics are beautiful...")
- Technical specs without purpose ("1920x1080 resolution, dynamic shadows...")
- Personal taste instead of analysis ("I don't like pixel art")
- Missing the point of stylization ("Realistic is better")

---

### Sound & Audio Design: Music, Effects & Sonic Language

**Strong Examples:**
- **Outer Wilds** – Analyzes music as narrative, explains silence as design choice
- **DOOM (2016)** – Shows how audio rhythm locks to gameplay mechanics

**What NOT to Do:**
- Generic emotional description ("The music makes you feel epic")
- Surface-level facts ("50 tracks, orchestral, composed by...")
- Personal taste as analysis ("The music got boring")
- Dismissing audio entirely

---

### Verdict & Recommendation: Synthesizing into Judgment

**Strong Examples:**
- **A Short Hike** – Synthesizes central thesis, makes specific recommendations, ends memorably
- **Elden Ring** – Connects all analysis to one idea, honest about limitations, specific about audience

**What NOT to Do:**
- Generic conclusion ("This is a really good game, 10/10")
- Hedging instead of judgment ("I think people might kind of like it")
- Recapping instead of synthesizing
- Dismissive gatekeeping

---

## How Students Use This

### Before:
Students read ONE strong example and had to guess how to improve their own writing.

### Now:
1. **See what works** – Strong examples with clear explanation of why they work
2. **See what doesn't** – Specific examples of mistakes to avoid
3. **Understand the difference** – Side-by-side comparison of good vs. weak writing
4. **Practice** – Guided exercises to apply the learning

---

## Example Structure

Each section now follows this pattern:

```
STRONG EXAMPLE #1: [Game Title]
- Full passage from a strong review
- Bulleted explanation of why it works

STRONG EXAMPLE #2: [Game Title]
- Another strong example showing different approach
- Explanation of its strengths

WEAK EXAMPLES: What NOT to Do
- Weak Example #1: [Problem type] + explanation
- Weak Example #2: [Problem type] + explanation
- Weak Example #3: [Problem type] + explanation
- Weak Example #4: [Problem type] + explanation

EXPLANATION
- Comprehensive breakdown of what works and why

PRACTICE
- Guided exercises with Weak/Strong contrasts
```

## Learning Progression

### Student's Journey Through One Section:

1. **Strong Examples** – See what professionals actually write
   - Two different approaches to the same section
   - Clear explanation of why each works

2. **What NOT to Do** – Avoid these common pitfalls
   - Four weak examples representing different mistakes
   - Explanation of what makes each one fail

3. **Explanation** – Understand the principles
   - Why professional analysis works
   - What separates good from mediocre
   - Key insights about the medium

4. **Practice** – Apply the learning
   - Guided prompts comparing weak vs. strong
   - Practical exercises
   - Scaffolding for their own writing

## Summary

The Training Quest went from:
- One example per section
- General explanations
- "Here's what to do" guidance

To:
- Two strong examples per section (different approaches)
- Four weak examples per section (mistakes to avoid)
- Clear "why this works" / "why this fails" explanations
- Comparative learning (weak vs. strong)
- Specific game examples students can reference

**Result:** Students now have a complete understanding of not just WHAT good analysis is, but EXACTLY how it differs from weak analysis.
